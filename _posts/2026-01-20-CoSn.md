[cite_start]**Kagome-lattice metals** like CoSn and FeSn have recently become a hot topic in condensed matter physics[cite: 45]. [cite_start]Their unique geometry—a network of corner-sharing triangles—hosts electronic "flat bands" and topological phases that promise exciting new physical properties[cite: 19, 45, 52].

[cite_start]However, our understanding of these materials has largely relied on their **average crystal structure**—the long-range, periodic arrangement of atoms[cite: 20, 55]. [cite_start]In our recent paper published in the *Journal of the American Chemical Society*, we reveal that this global picture might be missing crucial details hidden at the local scale[cite: 21, 56].

> [cite_start]**Key Finding:** We report a Fe-doping induced co-emergence of antiferromagnetic (AFM) order and local symmetry breaking in $(Co_{0.45}Fe_{0.55})Sn$[cite: 21, 106].

## 1. The "Average" Picture: Hexagonal and Magnetic

[cite_start]Using **neutron diffraction**, we first looked at the long-range order of the material[cite: 100]. [cite_start]We confirmed that below the Neél temperature ($T_N \approx 140$ K), the material develops an **A-type antiferromagnetic (AFM) order**[cite: 21, 108].

[cite_start]In this state, the magnetic moments on the Kagome layer align ferromagnetically within the plane but stack antiferromagnetically along the *c*-axis[cite: 21].

[cite_start]Globally, the crystal structure appears to remain stable in its hexagonal **$P6/mmm$** symmetry[cite: 196, 201]. [cite_start]If we only looked at standard diffraction, we would think the lattice is perfectly hexagonal down to the lowest temperatures[cite: 202, 203].

## 2. The "Local" Picture: Hidden Orthorhombic Distortion

[cite_start]To see what was happening at the atomic scale, we employed **Synchrotron X-ray Pair Distribution Function (PDF)** analysis and **Reverse Monte Carlo (RMC)** modeling[cite: 111, 119]. [cite_start]Unlike standard diffraction, PDF allows us to see local deviations from the average structure[cite: 43].

[cite_start]We discovered that below $T_N$, the *local* symmetry breaks from hexagonal to **orthorhombic ($Cmmm$)**[cite: 107].

[cite_start]This distortion is subtle—long-range diffraction averages it out—but it represents a fundamental instability in the lattice that "turns on" only when the magnetic order sets in[cite: 109, 296].

## 3. The Mechanism: Sn(2) Atoms on the Move

What drives this local symmetry breaking? [cite_start]Our analysis points to the **Sn(2) atoms** located in the honeycomb layers sandwiched between the magnetic Kagome layers[cite: 107, 418].

[cite_start]RMC modeling reveals that these Sn(2) atoms shift off-axis, moving perpendicular to the crystalline $a/b$ direction[cite: 107]. [cite_start]This displacement creates locally varied bond lengths and angles between the magnetic metal ions (Co/Fe) and the Sn(2) atoms[cite: 110].

[cite_start]We argue that this structural distortion has a **magnetic origin**[cite: 38]. [cite_start]The out-of-plane magnetic exchange interaction ($J_c$)—which couples the magnetic layers via the $M-Sn(2)-M$ pathway—likely drives these local displacements to stabilize the AFM ground state[cite: 38, 502, 507].

## Why This Matters

[cite_start]This study highlights a strong, previously overlooked coupling between **spin and lattice degrees of freedom** in Kagome metals[cite: 504].

[cite_start]Theoretical calculations for these materials often assume a perfect hexagonal lattice[cite: 55]. [cite_start]However, the existence of local orthorhombic distortions could significantly impact the electronic band structure, potentially altering the behavior of the much-sought-after flat bands[cite: 508].

[cite_start]By combining neutron diffraction (to see the "average" structure) with total scattering (to see the "local" structure), we can unveil hidden complexities in functional materials that standard techniques might miss[cite: 106].
